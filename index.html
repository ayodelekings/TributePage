Funmilayo Ransome-Kuti

The Nigerian educator, political campaigner, women's rights activist and traditional aristocrat

Here's a timeline of Funmilayo Ransome-Kuti's life:

  • 1900 - Born in Abeokuta, Nigeria
  • 1914 - Attended Abeokuta Grammar School for her secondary school education as one of the first female students
  • 1925 - Got married to Reverend Israel Oludotun Ransome-Kuti
  • 1928 - Established one of the first preschool classes in Nigeria, followed by a club for young women of elite families to encourage "self-improvement" and also literacy classes for women with less education
  • 1932 - Helped establish the Abeokuta Ladies CLub which focused on charity work, sewing, catering and adult education classes
  • 1935-1936 - Alongside her husband, they bought and shipped a secondhand car from England. She was the first woman to drive a car in Abeokuta and possibly in Nigeria
  • 1944 - Developed a successful campaign to stop local authorities seizing rice from market women under false pretenses
  • 1946 - The club was formally renamed the Abeokuta Women's Union
  • 1949 - Ransome-Kuti proposed the creation of the Nigerian Women's Union (NWU) in order to better support women's right and enfranchisement across the country
  • 1953 - Organized a conference in Abeokuta to discuss women's suffrage and political representation
  • 1956 - In China, gave public lectures on NIgerian women and culture and met Mao Zedong
  • 1965 - Received the national honour of membership in the Order of the Niger
  • 1968 - Was bestowed an honorary doctorate of laws by the University of Ibadan
  • 1969 - Was appointed chairman of the Advisory Board of Education by the western Nigeria state government and served as a consultant to the Federal Ministry of Education
  • 1970 - Received the Lenin Peace Price
  • 1978 - Died at the age of 77years after being hospitalized caused by an attack while visiting her son at Kalakuta Republic

A trailblazer in many ways, Ransome-Kuti was also the first Nigerian woman to drive a car. She was also the only woman in Nigeria’s 1947 delegation to London, which lodged a protest and set the nation on the path toward self-government. As one of the few women elected to Nigeria’s house of chiefs, she was recognized for her advocacy work on behalf of women's rights and education, and revered as the “Lioness of Lisabi” and the “Mother of Africa.”
